The Metal Knight of the 80s

In the '80s and '90s, America dreamt in Technicolor.

Everything was over the top: the hair, the muscles, the practical effects ‚Äî and, of course, the global threats. Sci-fi reigned supreme, flirting with chaos and the impossible. The apocalypse was pop. Communism, the big boss villain. And aliens? Always ready for a hostile spin on Earth. In this bipolar cinematic world, where the future was always gray and radioactive, Hollywood birthed robots, cyborgs, and traumatized ex-soldiers as if birthing humanity's destiny itself. In the middle of it all, a metal knight emerges: Robocop. Robocop was born from a script nobody wanted to touch.

A technological zombie in a Detroit uniform with the soul of a Western. The script was bad, nobody liked it, directors vanished, and actors turned up their noses at the idea of spending two hours with a bucket on their head. Schwarzenegger almost became the cop, but even he understood that a gym locker inside an armor suit would turn into a joke. Who saved the project was director Paul Verhoeven's wife. Yeah, she saw the poetry where he only saw shootouts. She realized that, behind the explosions, there was an essay on identity, consumerism, and privatized justice. Bingo. The movie went from trash to history. Peter Weller was the chosen one.

Not because he was the favorite, but because he was all that was left. He suffered on set. The Robocop suit was a walking sauna made by Rob Bottin, the obsessed genius of practical effects. Nothing worked. And Verhoeven roared on set as if directing an alien opera. Nancy Allen almost quit on the first day, thinking she'd accepted a role in a B-movie directed by a lunatic. Even so, the movie became a classic.

But not without a fight. Censorship immediately slapped it with an X ‚Äî not the "X" of Xavier, but the X for forbidden film. Verhoeven had to cut scene by scene, blood by blood, until he got that "R" rating. Still, Robocop premiered on the same day as Jaws 4, and even with fewer theaters and less money, it bit off more than the retired shark competition. And the box office?

A chaotic accounting mess. Orion, a small studio, sold international rights to everyone. Nobody knows how much the movie truly made. But it turned a profit. So much profit that they ordered an animated series and greenlit a sequel. Then the nightmare called "Robocop 2" began. And today?

Final Analytical Wrap-Up:

Verhoeven bailed. Schwarzenegger called him to direct Total Recall with total freedom. He jumped at the chance. The sequel fell into the wrong hands. Frank Miller's script was butchered. The director fought with the producer. The protagonist suffered in the suit. And the movie, despite making money, disappointed. Then came 3... and that was the end.
